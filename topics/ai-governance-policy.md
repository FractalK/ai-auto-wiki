AI governance refers to the institutional, legal, and international mechanisms through which AI development and deployment are regulated, made accountable, and bounded. As of late 2025, no comprehensive national or international AI governance framework is in force. The dominant political argument against regulation — that slowing domestic AI development cedes advantage to adversaries — parallels the logic that prevented early regulation of social media, which Harris argues produced a demonstrable weakening of democratic institutions and mental health outcomes.

Harris's framework identifies four regulatory red lines: mass labor displacement without transition infrastructure, AI-enabled surveillance states that permanently erode privacy, engagement-maximizing AI companions for minors, and deployment of uncontrollable superintelligent systems. These are proposed as the minimum scope for international coordination, analogous to the nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty and the Montreal Protocol on ozone-depleting chemicals — precedents where countries with adversarial relationships nonetheless coordinated on shared existential risks.

Enforcing AI governance agreements faces challenges specific to the technology: unlike nuclear detonations, AI training runs are not easily monitored externally. However, researchers cited as the "AI-27 authors" argue that monitoring approximately 95% of global compute — through satellite heat-emission monitoring, electrical signal tracking, and shared data center mapping — would be sufficient for treaties to be enforceable, because only actors with substantial compute fractions can build the most dangerous systems. The 2023–2024 Biden-Xi meetings produced an initial agreement, at China's request, to prevent AI from being used in nuclear command and control systems — a precedent that adversarial powers can coordinate on AI safety when mutual existential stakes are recognized.
